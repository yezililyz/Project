Flagship Project

Proof of Match: AI-Powered Deal-Ready Networking

1. Problem Statement

Traditional event networking relies on shallow keyword signals. In founder, investor, and operator communities, matching the right people is hard, and proving credibility is even harder. Warm intros are opaque, deal‑flow is fragmented, and a lot of high‑potential conversations never happen because the right signals are missing or buried. An investor and a founder might share a "DeFi" tag but have deep mismatches in funding stage, mandate, or operational readiness, resulting in low-signal conversations that waste high-value time.Event organizers feel the churn pressure and lack of insight brining sponsor ROI to YoY revenue growth signal. With Proof of Match, I wanted to explore how to design a system that makes matching more intentional, more transparent, and easier to act on, without turning it into a shallow “networking app”

2. Research & Insights

Through interviews, LinkedIn how‑tos, and founder playbooks, I identified that deal-ready meetings require complementarity (offer vs. need) rather than just similarity. I mapped JTBD for three personas: Investors (thesis-fit pipeline), Founders (strategic capital), and Regulators (policy engagement). And researched the current gap of existing tooling for capital networking organizer complaining the ROI is fuzzy and organizers struggle to attribute meetings to business results.

3. Solution & Process

Architected a 6-agent AI pipeline with fact signals that enriches raw data, extracts traction facts, and scores pairs across 7 independent dimensions: Persona Fit, Complementary Value, Intent Overlap, Sector Overlap, Stage Fit, Credibility, and Non-Obviousness matching.

4. Impact & Metrics

Delivered an intelligence engine that generates prioritized "Match Cards" with natural language reasoning. The system successfully surfaces non-obvious connections—detecting when a fintech founder and a healthcare regulator are solving the same protocol challenges.

5. Reflection

The core challenge was balancing semantic flexibility with deterministic filters, a classical tradepff between deterministic and probabilistic pipeline in traditional ML and LLM outcomes. By separating 'who you are' from 'what you want' in the embedding space, we avoided the 'echo chamber' effect of similarity-only matching. Next iterations would focus on incorporating real-time feedback loops from scheduled meeting outcomes

Go1: Retention via Content Intelligence

$1.7M ARR Retained
+35% Playability Lift

1. Problem: Go1 had a vast B2B learning catalog, but learners and customer admins struggled to quickly find the right content for their goals, leading to low engagement and underused contracts. The challenge was to turn a noisy, fragmented content universe into a guided discovery experience that could surface high‑value learning paths for the right users at the right time. Technical failures and poor metadata were blocking users from accessing curated content, leading to churn in the enterprise sector.Fragmented event tracking across mobile and desktop platforms made it impossible to isolate true activation/engagement signals from noise for 8M+ users

2. Research: I partnered with customer success and sales to collect qualitative feedback on how L&D teams curated content manually, what “good fit” meant in their context, and which gaps generated the most friction. This work surfaced two core needs: trustable content quality signals (credibility, freshness, relevance) and faster ways to assemble role and skill‑specific learning journeys without starting from scratch. I then conducted quantitative behavioral funnel analysis and synthesized qualitative insights with quantitative engagement data. Analyzed search-to-play rates as an activation proxy, identifying friction points in the content consumption funnel.

3. Solution: I worked with engineering and drove core product growth by building an LLM-powered metadata enrichment pipeline and rebuilding the learner activation and engagement layer. Architected global event schemas that cleanly isolated true learner and content-partner activation signals from noise, and redesigned the content partner upload workflow to unlock higher-quality supply and faster time-to-value.

4. Impact: Yielded +25% search discoverability and +15% content consumption, retaining $1.7M in ARR.

5. Reflection: Found that "content playability" is often a data-integrity issue rather than a UX issue. Automated metadata enrichment is the only way to scale curation for enterprise-grade learning libraries.

Dailymotion: User insight led to platform revamp & Global Taxonomy Redesign

+58% Search Views
+25% Revenue Lift

1. Problem: New mobile users were overwhelmingly starting with search (around 90% of first sessions), but barely 60% of those searches led to a video play in the same session, exposing a weak first-touch experience and poor perceived relevance. Inconsistent, fragmented tracking between mobile and desktop made it impossible to reliably tie UI and search changes to downstream impact across a 400M+ user base.

2. Research: I combined structured user interviews with funnel and cohort analysis to understand how Gen Z users searched, skimmed results, and decided whether to commit to a watch. Segmenting by new vs. returning users and acquisition channel, I traced where they were leaking along search‑to‑play, session depth, and repeat‑view patterns. This analysis surfaced three core friction points: relevance and topic understanding in search results, a watch page that didn’t quickly validate their choice, and limited visibility into which user paths translated into sustained engagement.

3. Solution: I designed and deployed a unified Amplitude event model and cross‑platform dashboards that connected acquisition sources to activation (search‑to‑play), engagement (session depth, repeat views), and retention, creating a trustworthy instrumentation layer for experimentation. In parallel, I led a cross‑functional initiative to revamp the watch‑page UX to better reinforce relevance and next actions, migrate to a stronger search provider, and introduce Google NLP–powered topic annotation to enable more semantic search and ranking. These changes were embedded into an A/B testing and tracking framework so every search or watch‑page iteration could be evaluated against the specific funnel breaks we had identified. .

4. Impact: This work increased overall engagement by 17%, boosted search‑to‑play conversion by 25%, and drove a 50% uplift in views originating from search. At the same time, more efficient infrastructure and query behavior contributed to roughly a 20% reduction in related infra costs.

5. Reflection: Durable growth depends on clean taxonomy and reliable instrumentation.By reframing Dailymotion’s rebrand as a mobile‑first, Gen Z‑heavy challenge and using search‑to‑play as the primary activation lens, we were able to align UX, search relevance, and data infrastructure around a single narrative: turning high‑intent search sessions into meaningful, repeatable engagement.

BlaBlaCar: Growth Lifecycle Experiments

+15% Return trip booking
+20% User activation
+30% Budget saved

1. Problem: Return‑trip booking rates were lagging and marketing spend was inefficient across 9 markets because performance was measured on last‑click attribution, masking true multimodal behaviour and underestimating BlaBlaCar’s role in the journey.

2. Research: I analysed return‑trip funnels across BlaBlaCar and BlaBlaBus surfaces and performed proxy analysis to identify gaps in return behaviour. By mapping user journeys and intermodal behaviour (bus ↔ carpool), I found that roughly 9% of users booked outbound and return on different modalities; once controlled for this, the apparent 12‑point gap in return bookings shrank to around 3 points. This reframed the core question from “fix BlaBlaBus return bookings” to “increase the share of any return trip booked on BlaBlaCar, regardless of modality, by at least 3 percentage points.

3. Solution: I architected a growth lifecycle experiments system focused on return trips during the integration of BlaBlaBus, where the experience and data were fragmented across three surfaces (BlaBlaCar, a white‑label bus flow, and a standalone BlaBlaBus app). On the product side, I introduced clearer return‑trip prompts and built cohort logic that segmented users by outbound modality (bus vs. carpool). On the CRM side, I partnered with ops to design Braze journeys: event‑triggered re‑engagement flows with timing tuned to when each cohort historically booked returns (e.g., a ~24h window for carpool users and a much tighter same‑session window for bus users). Treating tracking as a blocking dependency, I defined outbound/return booking events and CRM triggers, then regression‑tested the entire stack by walking journeys end‑to‑end, validating that events fired with correct properties, landed in analytics and Braze, and appeared accurately in measurement dashboards. On the automation side, I partnered with automation team implemented a probabilistic multi‑touch attribution model (Markov‑chain–style) that redistributed credit across the full journey instead of giving it all to the final touch. The updated channel weights were then wired back into campaign rules and reporting, so budget decisions and performance reviews reflected this more realistic view of impact.

4. Impact: Validated +15% conversion lift in return bookings and improved marketing ops efficiency by 30%.

5. Reflection: Transitioning from simple re-engagement to event-triggered activation sequences requires tight coordination between marketing and product data layers.

Sparteo: Revenue Safeguarding & Privacy

15% Revenue Protected

1. Problem: Tightening GDPR/CNIL rules made our cross‑platform tracking and reporting non‑compliant for a growing chunk of traffic, creating sudden drops in publisher KPIs and real risk of “invisible” consent losses in their numbers.

2. Research: Mapped the full data path (CMP → Sparteo → SSP/DSP → reports), benchmarked against Google Ads–style consent handling, and worked with legal, CSM, and engineering to quantify how consent vs non‑consent traffic should appear in metrics like sessions, RPM, and revenue.

3. Solution: Designed and shipped a consent‑aware reporting model: non‑consent traffic is aggregated (no URL/user‑level detail), user‑level KPIs are recalculated on consented sessions only, and we updated in‑product copy plus CSM playbooks so publishers clearly understand why RPM per session and traffic counts shift under CNIL‑compliant rules.

4. Impact: Successfully safeguarded ~15% of annual revenue through compliant data infrastructure.

5. Reflection: Privacy isn't just a compliance hurdle—it's a product feature that builds long-term trust with publishers and ecosystem partners.Treating privacy as a first‑class product constraint forced us to rebuild metrics around truth instead of convenience, which strengthened publisher trust in Sparteo’s numbers and made future compliance changes easier to land.
